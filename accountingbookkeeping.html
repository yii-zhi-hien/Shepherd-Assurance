Financial Reporting (Unaudited Financial Statements)

We provide expert compilation report preparation to assess and showcase your business's financial performance.

Financial reporting is a critical function for any business, offering key insights into its financial health and position. These reports not only help business owners, investors, and other stakeholders understand the company's financial standing but also play a vital role in making informed decisions that impact the business's future growth and sustainability.

Unaudited financial statements are an essential tool in financial reporting. They summarize a company’s financial status but have not undergone the formal audit process by an external party. While they are not as rigorously tested as audited statements, unaudited reports provide a snapshot of financial performance, and they serve the needs of businesses that require timely and detailed insights but do not necessarily require an official audit.

These unaudited financial statements typically include the following:
  • Income Statement (Profit and Loss Statement): This document provides a detailed summary of your business’s revenues, expenses, and profits over a specific period. It helps you gauge profitability and identify trends in income and expenditure. By analyzing this statement, business owners can make informed decisions about pricing, cost control, and strategic financial planning.
  • Balance Sheet: A balance sheet offers a snapshot of your business’s financial condition at a specific point in time. It lists assets, liabilities, and equity, giving stakeholders a clear understanding of what the company owns (assets), owes (liabilities), and the residual interest of the owners (equity). This document is vital for assessing the financial stability and liquidity of the business.
  • Cash Flow Statement: This statement tracks the movement of cash into and out of the business during a given period. By breaking down cash flows into operations, investing, and financing activities, it offers transparency into how the business is managing its liquidity. A solid understanding of cash flow is crucial for ensuring the business can meet its financial obligations and sustain operations.
  • Statement of Changes in Equity: This statement outlines the changes in the company’s equity over a specified period. It typically includes profits, distributions, dividends, and other factors affecting the owner’s equity. This document is essential for understanding how the company’s value evolves over time and the factors contributing to that growth or decline.
  • Notes to the Financial Statements: These notes offer additional context and explanations for the financial data presented in the main financial statements. They might include details on accounting methods, significant transactions, contingencies, or assumptions that impact the business's financial standing. These notes are vital for providing a deeper understanding of the financials and ensuring transparency.

Why are Unaudited Financial Statements Important?

Unaudited financial statements are particularly useful for small to medium-sized businesses or startups that need to maintain transparency with investors, creditors, and internal stakeholders. They are not as detailed or independent as audited statements but still offer significant value:

  • Internal decision-making: Business owners and management rely on these reports to make informed decisions about operations, expansion, and budgeting.
  • External stakeholder communication: Investors, lenders, and partners often request unaudited statements to evaluate the financial health of a business before making investment or loan decisions.
  • Compliance and tax filings: While these reports are not audited, they can still be used for tax preparation and to comply with industry regulations.

Our compilation report preparation service ensures that these statements are prepared accurately, in a timely manner, and tailored to your business's specific needs. These detailed and reliable financial reports will help you maintain a clear picture of your business’s financial health and can be an invaluable tool for future planning, budgeting, and attracting potential investors.

eXtensible Business Reporting Language (XBRL)

We provide preparation of eXtensible Business Reporting Language – XBRL conversion. All Singapore (SG) incorporated companies are required to file financial statements (FS) with ACRA, except for those which are exempted. Some companies will file a full set of FS in XBRL format, while others will file key financial data in XBRL format along with a full set of signed copies of the FS tabled at the annual general meeting and/or circulated to members (AGM FS) in PDF format. If you own a sole proprietorship, partnership, limited liability partnership, or limited partnership, you are not required to file FS with ACRA. For the avoidance of doubt, all SG incorporated companies are required to prepare financial statements, except for those which are dormant relevant companies.

Revised XBRL filing requirements

ACRA has revised the filing requirements and data elements in XBRL format for companies. This is a part of our efforts to streamline the filing of FS. The effective implementation date for companies to file FS in XBRL format using the revised filing requirements and data elements are as follows:

  • Companies are required to apply the revised filing requirements and data elements on or after 1 May 2021;
  • Companies can opt to voluntarily apply the revised filing requirements and data elements from 16 May 2020 to 30 April 2021 (both dates inclusive).

Our firm have provided 2 templates which to be used by mostly the Companies to meet the revised filing requirements and data elements:

  • Full XBRL template – The number of data elements for this template has been reduced by 50% to about 210 data elements. It will capture the information in primary statements and selected notes to FS.
  • Simplified XBRL template - This template, which replaces XBRL FSH (General), has about 120 data elements. It will capture the complete information in the statements of financial performance and position.

Reconciliation Services

We provide reconciliation services to ensure the management accounts prepared have been improved accuracy and integrity, fraud prevention as well as compliance the financial reporting standards. Reconciliation services are a critical aspect of financial management that ensure the accuracy and integrity of a company’s financial records. They involve comparing two sets of records — typically an internal record (like the company’s accounting ledger) and an external source (like a bank statement) — to ensure they match and to identify any discrepancies that need to be resolved.

Types of Reconciliation Services:
  1. Bank Reconciliation

    This is the process of matching the company’s internal financial records with the transactions recorded by the bank. It helps ensure that the cash balance in the company's ledger is accurate and reflects what’s in the bank.

  2. Credit Card Reconciliation

    This is the process of ensuring that all company credit card transactions are correctly recorded in the financial system. It's similar to bank reconciliation but specifically for credit card statements.

  3. Accounts Receivable and Accounts Payable Reconciliation

    Ensures that the balances in the accounts receivable (money owed to the company) and accounts payable (money the company owes) are accurate and up to date.

  4. Asset Reconciliation

    This process ensures that the company’s recorded asset values (e.g., property, plant, equipment, or intangible assets) match physical or third-party records, including insurance policies.

  5. Loan or Debt Reconciliation

    This type of reconciliation is used to ensure that loans, lines of credit, or other financial liabilities are properly recorded and that the company's records match the lender’s statements.

  6. Sales Reconciliation

    Ensures that sales recorded in the company’s books match the actual sales revenue generated, often used in retail, online businesses, and service-oriented companies.

  7. Investment Portfolio Reconciliation

    This involves reconciling the company's investment portfolio (e.g., stocks, bonds, mutual funds) to ensure that the recorded values are accurate and align with third-party statements (from brokers or investment managers).

  8. General Ledger Reconciliation

    This service involves reconciling the balances in the general ledger to ensure they are accurate and complete. It typically involves reconciling sub-ledgers (such as accounts payable and accounts receivable) with the general ledger.

  9. Inventory Reconciliation

    Ensures that the inventory records in the company's books match the physical inventory count. This is crucial for businesses that deal with goods and materials.

  10. Payroll Reconciliation

    Ensures that the payroll expenses and liabilities (e.g., taxes withheld) are recorded correctly and that the amounts in the company's records match with the actual disbursements.

  11. Intercompany Reconciliation

    This process is used by companies with multiple subsidiaries or branches, ensuring that intercompany transactions (such as transfers of goods, services, or funds) are correctly recorded across all entities.

Reconciliation services are a vital part of maintaining accurate financial records. By regularly reconciling various accounts, businesses can ensure that their financial reporting is accurate, that they are compliant with tax and regulatory requirements, and that they have a clear picture of their financial health. Different types of reconciliation can be outsourced to specialized firms or handled by internal teams depending on the size and complexity of the organization. Regular reconciliations help prevent errors, reduce fraud risks, improve financial management, and facilitate timely decision-making.

Cash Flow Management

Cash flow management is the process of monitoring, analyzing, and optimizing the flow of cash in and out of a business. Effective cash flow management ensures that a company can meet its financial obligations, invest in growth, and avoid liquidity issues, which are critical for the ongoing health of a business.

Key Elements of Cash Flow Management:

  1. Cash Inflows
    • Sales Revenue: The primary source of cash flow for most businesses, coming from selling products or services.
    • Loans or Credit: Cash obtained from external sources, such as bank loans, lines of credit, or investor funding.
    • Investments: Cash from investments or the sale of assets.
    • Other Income: Includes other forms of income like interest, royalties, or government grants.
  2. Cash Outflows
    • Operating Expenses: Day-to-day expenses needed to run the business, including salaries, rent, utilities, and office supplies.
    • Cost of Goods Sold (COGS): The cost of materials, labor, and manufacturing required to produce goods or services.
    • Debt Payments: Payments made toward loans, credit lines, or interest on debt.
    • Capital Expenditures: Investments in long-term assets like property, equipment, or technology.
    • Taxes and Compliance Costs: Government levies, social security contributions, and other regulatory expenses.
  3. Net Cash Flow

    The difference between total cash inflows and total cash outflows over a specific period.

    • Positive Cash Flow: Indicates that a business is generating more cash than it is spending, which allows for reinvestment, debt reduction, or savings.
    • Negative Cash Flow: Indicates that a business is spending more cash than it is receiving, which could lead to liquidity problems or the need for financing.

Cash flow management is essential for the survival and growth of any business. By implementing strategies such as optimizing accounts receivable, controlling costs, maintaining a cash reserve, and forecasting cash flow, businesses can ensure they have enough liquidity to meet their obligations, invest in growth, and weather financial challenges. Effective cash flow management enhances a business's ability to plan, execute, and expand, all while maintaining financial stability.
